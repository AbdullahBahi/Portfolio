Simple Momentum Strategy - Backtesting¶

In this notebook, we will try to answer a very simple question, and that is, can we generate profit by trading stocks at daily basis based on past day's return? meaning that if the return of a stock in the past day is positive, we buy the stock, and if it's negative, we just don't take any actions.

Strategy Logic

  • At the end of each day, we check the day returns of at least 10 stocks (depending on the availability of data).
  • We buy the stocks with positive returns at the closing price of the session, and ignore the stocks with negative rturns. The opened positions are equally weighted.
  • The positions are kept opened overnight and throughout the next trading session.
  • All opened positions are closed at the end of the next trading session, at the closing price.

Important Note: Opening positions at the end of the previous day's session ensures that positions take advantage of any overnight news, in contrast with opening the positions at the open price of the same day.

We will use the current constituents of the EGX30 index as our universe of stocks for backtesting our strategy, and we will go back in time and start from the oldest day possible that satisfies the 10 stocks rule.

1.3. Calculating historical daily returns¶

We now proceed to calculate the historical daily returns for the stocks and benchmark, utilizing the following formula:

$$ r_i(t) = \frac{p_i(t) - p_i(t-1)}{p_i(t-1)} $$

where:

  • $r_i(t)$ is the return of stock $i$ at time $t$
  • $p_i(t)$ is the price of stock $i$ at time $t$ (selling price)
  • $p_i(t-1)$ is the price of stock $i$ at time $t-1$ (purchase price)

This is consitstent with the strategy logic mentioned before that we open positions at the close price of the previous day $t-1$. We could have used the open price at time $t$ as the purchase price, but this would ignore the effect of ov.ernight news

1.4. Filtering returns¶

In order to filter our returns from misleading data, we will apply the following filters:

  • Continuity filter: Only daily returns that are calculated using consecutive business days are kept.
  • Enough stocks filter: Only days with returns data for at least 10 stocks are considered.

2. Calculating Strategy Returns¶

Now that we have our returns calculated and filtered, we can proceed to calculating strategy returns.

2.1. Generating trading signals¶

First, we will generate the trading signals used to open positions using returns of the past business day.

$$ \text{signal}_i(t) = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } r_i(t-1) > 0 \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases} $$

where:

  • $signal_i(t)$ is the signal used for stock $i$ at time $t$
  • $r_i(t-1)$ is the return of stock $i$ at time $t-1$

# First, we will shift the returns one business day forward, this will make returns of time t-1 available at time t.

tuples = []

shifted_multi_index = pd.MultiIndex.from_tuples(tuples)
lagged_rets = rets.copy()

2.2. Calculating strategy returns (per position)¶

Now, we calculate the returns of our strategy at stocks level by multiplying the signals and stocks returns:

$$ r_{strategy,i}(t) = {signal_i(t)}*{r_i(t)} $$

where:

  • $r_{strategy,i}(t)$ is the return of stock $i$ at time $t$ if it was bought by the strategy.
  • $signal_i(t)$ is the signal used for stock $i$ at time $t$
  • $r_i(t-1)$ is the return of stock $i$ at time $t-1$

Then we drop all position with a return of 0 (i.e. the positions that weren't opened), which resulted from multiplication by 0 in the signals. This will leave us with returns of position we opened only.

## Calculate strategy returns

Before aggrigating returns for each day, we need to account for trading fees at both opening (buy) and closing (sell) transactions. The following table shows the transactions fees for each EGX transaction, disregarding brokerage fees, which we will ignore under the assumption that this strategy shall be implemented by licensed brokerage firm, with direct access to the exchange.

This makes the total transaction fees per order equan to 4bps, which we multiply by 2 to account for both opening and closing transactions, then we subtract it from the return of each position.

2.4. Calculating strategy returns (per day)¶

Now, we aggrigate the returns for each day by calculating the average return of positions every day. This is consistent with the strategy logic where we open equally weighted positions.

$$ r_{\text{strategy}}(t) = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N} r_{\text{strategy},i}(t)}{N} $$

where:

  • $r_{strategy}(t)$ is the average return of the strategy at time $t$.
  • $r_{strategy,i}(t)$ is the return of stock $i$ at time $t$ if it was bought by the strategy.

4. Conclusions¶

The results show that even a simple signal generated based on past day return can be used to construct a profitable algorithmic trading strategy that significantly outperforms the EGX30 index. This study does not imply that this specific strategy would be profitable, however, it shows the huge potential of algorithmic trading in the egyptian stock market, given that more sophisticated algorithms were used to generate signals, and more appropriate risk management mechanisms were used to manage strategy risk.
